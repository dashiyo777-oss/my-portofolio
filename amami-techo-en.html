TRENDING

What everyone's eating

The pipeline is now familiar: born somewhere, goes viral in Korea, then conquers the world.

THE ORIGINAL

Dubai chocolate

Created by Dubai brand FIX Dessert Chocolatier: a chocolate bar filled with pistachio cream and kadayif — shredded pastry threads so fine they're nicknamed "angel hair." The vivid green cross-section and the crunchy ASMR snap made it a TikTok phenomenon. Its roots lie in knafeh, a beloved Middle Eastern dessert of kadayif, cheese, and syrup.

Fun fact: in Japan, where kadayif is hard to find, home bakers famously recreate it using crispy fried noodles meant for a Nagasaki noodle dish — and it works surprisingly well.
THE 2026 SEQUELS

Dubai chocolate mochi & angel hair chocolate

The boom keeps evolving. Korea's Dubai chocolate mochi — chewy rice cake meets crunchy kadayif — topped teen trend surveys. Next in line: angel hair chocolate, built around pişmaniye, a Turkish confection like fine cotton candy. Crisp outside, cloud-soft inside.

The hit formula: one texture is nice, but two opposing textures in one bite — crunchy + chewy, crisp + fluffy — is what makes the brain fall in love.
ICE CREAM

The return of "heavy" ice cream

The pistachio wave hit ice cream too: ultra-dense pistachio gelato is everywhere. Meanwhile Turkey's stretchy ice cream dondurma is back in the spotlight — its elasticity comes from salep, a flour made from orchid roots. After years of "light and refreshing," the pendulum has swung back to rich, dense, and gloriously indulgent.

FRUIT FACTS

Fruit is stranger than you think

The fruit bowl on your table is full of secrets.

JAPAN-GROWN

A banana you can eat — peel and all

Japan's "Mongee Banana" is grown from seeds and saplings frozen to −60°C, then thawed — a technique called freeze-thaw awakening that lets this tropical plant thrive in Japan's climate. Grown domestically with no post-harvest chemicals, its peel is thin, tender, and completely edible.

Bonus: botanically, a banana is a berry. A strawberry, by definition, is not.
TASTE HACK

The fruit that makes lemons taste sweet

The miracle fruit of West Africa contains a protein called miraculin that binds to your tongue's sweetness receptors — and switches them on only when acid arrives. Eat one, then bite a lemon or passion fruit: it tastes like someone dusted it with sugar. The effect lasts 30 minutes to two hours.

Fun fact: the berry itself is barely sweet. It doesn't make food sweeter — it makes your brain believe it is. Chefs use it to build sugar-free dessert experiences.
PERSIMMON

"When persimmons turn red, doctors turn pale"

So goes the Japanese proverb — and it holds up: one persimmon carries roughly a full day's vitamin C. Another quirk: the astringent tannin in bitter persimmons doesn't disappear when they're dried — it just changes into a form your tongue can't detect. The bitterness isn't removed; it's hidden.

STRAWBERRY

The real strawberry "fruit" is the seeds on the outside

The juicy red part is technically a swollen stem tip. The true fruits are the tiny specks on the surface, each containing a seed. Japan also breeds white strawberries — varieties like "Scent of First Love" that barely produce red pigment: fully ripe in fragrance, snow-white in color.

PINEAPPLE

That tingle means the pineapple is eating you back

Fresh pineapple contains bromelain, an enzyme that breaks down protein. The tingling on your tongue is that enzyme mildly digesting your tongue's surface. It's also why pineapple tenderizes meat, and why fresh pineapple ruins gelatin desserts. Cooking deactivates the enzyme.

APPLE

The "honey core" in apples isn't actually sweet

Those translucent patches prized in Japanese apples are sorbitol — a sugar alcohol only about 60% as sweet as sugar. It appears when an apple is so ripe it can't convert nutrients into fructose anymore. The honey core isn't sweetness itself; it's a certificate of full ripeness.

GRAPES

The white powder on grapes is a good sign

That dusty coating is bloom — a waxy layer the fruit produces itself to seal in moisture and fend off disease. It's a mark of freshness, so wash it off only right before eating. (Chocolate turning white is also called "bloom," but that's fat or sugar recrystallizing — a different story.)

BANANA

The black spots are an invitation

Those dark speckles are sugar spots — the sign that starch has fully converted to sugar and sweetness has peaked. A green banana is starchy and mild; a spotted one is dessert. Few fruits let you taste an entire flavor journey in a single piece.

WATERMELON

Vegetable or fruit? Both, officially

In horticultural terms, watermelon, melon, and strawberry grow on herbaceous plants, making them vegetables. But markets and nutritionists treat them as fruit, so Japan's agriculture ministry uses the diplomatic term "fruit-like vegetables." Everyone wins. One theory says the stripes evolved as a billboard for birds — free seed delivery.

MANDARIN

Yes, eating too many mandarins turns your palms yellow

The pigment β-cryptoxanthin accumulates in the skin's outer layer if you overindulge — a harmless condition that fades once you slow down. The same pigment is being studied for its links to bone health, and Japan's satsuma mandarins contain some of the highest levels in the world.

STORIES

Famous scenes & funny tales

Sweets in literature, film, and history — some hilarious, some heartwarming.

LITERATURE

One madeleine launched a 4,000-page novel

Proust's In Search of Lost Time truly begins when the narrator tastes a madeleine dipped in tea and childhood memories come flooding back. The phenomenon — scent and taste unlocking vivid memory — is now called the Proust effect in psychology. A small sponge cake became a scientific term.

You have one too: everyone owns a taste that acts as a memory switch — a grandmother's cookie tin, a childhood candy, a festival snack.
FILM

The gelato scene that changed the law

In Roman Holiday, the princess eats gelato on the Spanish Steps — one of cinema's most imitated moments. Seventy years of tourists recreating it took a toll: Rome eventually banned eating and sitting on the Steps, with fines. Recreating the scene today costs considerably more than the gelato.

HISTORY, CORRECTED

"Let them eat cake" — she never said it

There is no record of Marie Antoinette ever saying it. The line appears in Rousseau's Confessions, attributed to "a great princess" — written when Antoinette was still a child in Austria. And the original word wasn't even cake: it was brioche. Revolutionary propaganda pinned the quote on her, and it stuck for 250 years.

LITERARY ECCENTRIC

The great novelist who put bean-paste buns on rice

Mori Ōgai — army surgeon and one of Japan's greatest writers — loved "manjū chazuke": a sweet bean-paste bun plopped onto rice, with green tea poured over it. Trained in bacteriology, he so feared raw food that he even ate fruit boiled. Cutting-edge 19th-century science, applied with total commitment, produced one of history's oddest dessert habits. Those who've tried it report it's "surprisingly okay."

NATIONAL DEBATE

Japan's 45-year chocolate civil war

Kinoko no Yama (chocolate "mushrooms," 1975) versus Takenoko no Sato (chocolate "bamboo shoots," 1979): which is superior has been a national running joke for nearly half a century. The maker eventually held official "general elections" drawing millions of votes — Team Bamboo won. Stoking a rivalry while selling both sides: peaceful, brilliant business.

ONLY IN JAPAN

The strawberry shortcake you know doesn't exist abroad

Fluffy sponge, whipped cream, glistening strawberries — that iconic cake is a uniquely Japanese evolution; Western "shortcake" is typically a crumbly biscuit. The "short" likely comes from old English for "crumbly" (as in shortening), not "quick to make." Eating strawberry shortcake at Christmas? Also purely Japanese. Your "classic" is someone else's exotic.

NO WAY HOME

Castella: the Portuguese cake Portugal doesn't have

Portuguese traders brought castella's ancestor to Nagasaki in the 1500s. But today, no cake called "castella" exists in Portugal — the name likely derives from "bread of Castile," and the moist, honeyed loaf sold in Japan was perfected there over centuries. A beloved cake with no home to return to.

NAME ORIGINS

Parfait means "perfect." Sundae means... Sunday

The parfait takes its name from the French for "perfect" — a supremely confident dessert. The sundae was born in America: when selling soda water on the Sabbath was restricted, shops served the ice cream and syrup without the soda — a "Sunday." The spelling changed to "sundae," the story goes, because naming a dessert after the holy day felt a bit cheeky.

PERSIMMON

The persimmon that comes in a wooden treasure box

Japan's most exclusive persimmon, Tenka Fubu, must pass strict sugar-content standards — top specimens reach a sugar level around 25 Brix, sweeter than most melons. The finest grade, "Tenkabito," is presented in a paulownia box, the same wood used to store kimono and swords. At the season's first auction, a pair has sold for hundreds of thousands of yen.

Why paulownia? The wood regulates humidity and repels insects — Japan's traditional vault for precious things. Some luxury fruit gifts use hinoki cypress boxes instead. Fruit treated like heirlooms: that's Japan's gift culture.
GRAPES

Ruby Roman: the $10,000 bunch

Developed over 14 years in Ishikawa Prefecture, each grape is bigger than a large coin. Size, weight, sugar, and color standards are ruthless — fail any one and it can't bear the name. First-auction prices have exceeded ¥1.5 million (over $10,000) for a single bunch, and saplings are strictly controlled to prevent the variety leaving the prefecture.

MELON

One vine, one melon

Japan's crown muskmelons are grown by the "one vine, one fruit" method: every other melon on the plant is culled so all nutrients pour into a single chosen fruit. And that beautiful netting? It's the scar tissue of the skin cracking as the flesh outgrows it, healing over like a lattice of tiny scabs. The pattern collectors prize is, literally, the art of wounds.

MANGO

The mango that decides its own harvest day

Miyazaki's "Egg of the Sun" mango isn't cut from the tree. Each fruit hangs in a small net and is harvested only when it ripens fully and drops on its own. Humans don't judge ripeness — the tree does. Add strict standards (15+ Brix, 350g+), and you get first-auction prices of hundreds of thousands of yen for a pair.

FIRST AUCTION

Why anyone pays $30,000 for two melons

Yubari melons have fetched ¥5 million for a pair at the season's first auction. It's not madness — it's "goshūgi sōba," Japan's celebratory-bid culture. The buyer pays a fortune as applause for the farmers and earns national news coverage; the region's brand value soars. The same ritual crowns the year's first tuna. A festival where fruit is the star.

TOKYO · NIHONBASHI

Sembikiya: the shop that turned fruit into gifts

Founded in 1834, Tokyo's Sembikiya began as a fruit stall and in the early Meiji era opened a "fruit dining room" — the ancestor of Japan's fruit parlor culture. Today its flagship displays muskmelons priced like jewelry, and its fruit sandwiches and parfaits still draw lines. This is ground zero for Japan treating fruit as treasure.

TOKYO · GINZA

Shiseido Parlour: the cosmetics giant that started with soda

Shiseido began as a Ginza pharmacy. In 1902 it installed one of Japan's first soda fountains, serving ice cream sodas when ice cream was an extravagant rarity. That corner grew into Shiseido Parlour, still beloved for its strawberry parfait — a dessert salon born inside a drugstore.

YOKOHAMA

The hotel that invented pudding à la mode (and two national dishes)

Pudding à la mode — custard pudding surrounded by ice cream, fruit, and cream — was created after WWII at Yokohama's Hotel New Grand to delight the wives of occupation officers. A Japanese-born "Western" dessert. The same kitchen also invented spaghetti Napolitan and rice doria: one hotel, three national comfort foods.

KYOTO · GION

Kagizen Yoshifusa: edible coolness since the Edo period

This Gion confectionery, founded in the mid-Edo era, is famous for kuzukiri: translucent ribbons of kudzu starch, made to order and swum through ice water, dipped in dark sugar syrup before eating. Beloved by geiko and literati for generations — the taste of a Kyoto summer.

NATIONWIDE

The fruit-kingdom map: an excuse to travel

Okayama for white peaches and muscat grapes; Yamagata for Sato Nishiki cherries (named after the farmer who bred them); Yamanashi, Japan's top producer of peaches and grapes; Wakayama for mandarins; Aomori for apples; Miyazaki for mangoes. Fully ripened fruit eaten at the source is a different food from the early-picked versions in stores. A fruit lover's itinerary is written by the harvest calendar.

Traveler's tip: skip the all-you-can-eat farms and hit the roadside farmers' markets for "imperfect" fully-ripe rejects — ugly, half the price, and often the best fruit you'll ever taste.

WAGASHI

Kohakutō: jewels you can eat

Agar and sugar are boiled, set, and then left for about a week while only the surface crystallizes — creating a shell that shatters delicately over a soft jelly heart. Held to the light, the shards glow like minerals. Japanese artisans layer colors into each piece; the internet calls them "edible gemstones," and it's not wrong.

30-MINUTE LIFESPAN

The raindrop cake that expires in 30 minutes

Yamanashi's mizu shingen mochi looks like a giant raindrop that somehow holds its shape. Barely more than water, it collapses at room temperature, so its shelf life is about 30 minutes — no takeout, no shipping, sold only at the shop on summer weekends. It went viral abroad as the "raindrop cake." Beauty that exists because it vanishes: very Japanese, very cherry-blossom.

CROSS-SECTION ART

Fruit sandwiches are engineered, not sliced

The flower-like cross-section of a Japanese fruit sandwich is no accident: every strawberry's orientation, every kiwi's angle, the cream's thickness — all are arranged in reverse from the exact line of the future cut. Japan even coined a word for the pleasure: "moe-dan," roughly "cross-section love." Hours of hidden design for one perfect reveal — a close cousin of bento culture.

PASTRY CRAFT

Mirror cakes and sugar sculpture

A mirror glaze so glossy you can check your hair in it is pure temperature control — chocolate and gelatin poured at precisely the right degree. Competition sugar sculpture is more brutal still: pulling 160°C molten sugar nearly bare-handed, shaping flowers and birds in the seconds before it hardens. Behind every beautiful dessert stands physics, chemistry, and a pastry chef flirting with burns.
